WNM 695 01: Automotive UI

Problem

As the age of the autonomous car is coming and the ongoing integration of advanced computer systems into vehicles, the interfaces within are becoming ever-more complex.
Traditionally, we have the dashboard, central console, and infotainment screen inside a car. But for the self-driving car, the dashboard becomes less important, drivers or passengers will mainly focus on the central console and infotainment screen. The car interior and the HMI need a redesign to match the self-driving future.

Solution

As a legacy of history, the knobs and switches in the central console in the future will be replaced by a touchscreen. And the dashboard would not take the first priority in a self-driving car.
So it’s time to merge the dashboard, central console, and infotainment screen into one touchscreen.

User Flow

Josh got into the car, using his finger to unlock the screen and checking the car status, later he started the power and set his destination on the Velite Map. The car set out with autopilot.

During the trip, he opened the Spotify app to enjoy music while checking his Gmail on the infotainment screen.

When he arrived at the destination, he turned off the lights and shut down the car then left it charging in the parking lot.
Steve unlocked the car, returning home after a whole day work. He set his home to be the destination and switched the driving mode to the sports mode to make it move faster.

Steve got a notification from the car. It was his friend sending him a video call invitation. Steve accepted and joined the video call with his friend.

After he finished the call, Steve turned down the lights in the car and leaned to the back to start reading. He would arrive home in 15 minutes.
Julia listened to Spotify while driving on the road, she felt tired some minutes later, so she turned on the autopilot to let the car drive itself.

She opened the YouTube app and started to watch videos.

The car was driving out of the city and entered the forest, Julia noticed the beautiful scenery outside of the windows. So she opened the City View app and triggered the camera to record the wonderful scenery. After the recording finished, she uploaded the video to YouTube as a 360° video.

Final Design

-

Infotainment Screen

By using the right font size on the A4 paper size screen, contents should be readable while driving. The homepage is divided into two parts, and the “Sneak Peek” app on the top can be fixed when the user scrolls the screen. The app also gives the driver quick access to the most functions, while at the same time, the user can still customize the screen by dragging other apps to the top.

Final Design

-

Central Console Screen

The central console screen should not distract the driver and also has to be easy and quick to be controlled. The information structure of the system is set to be as simple as possible. Therefore, the driver can control the car in a smoother way with only one step.

The Smartphone App

In order to enable a full mobile experience of autonomous car use, we also built the smartphone app to cooperate with the HMI. Users could use their smartphones to check the car status, control part of exterior and interior features. It makes the smartphone to be a key of the car.

Wireframe

The structure of the smartphone app is simple. It has three sections. The status, exterior control, and interior control.

User Flow

Josh got up in the morning, picked up his phone, opened the app to connect his car. He checked the car status and started to heat up the seat.

After he finished breakfast, he opened the app and clicked the “pick up” icon, the car arrived in front of his door.

Josh set out with his car waiting for him outside, he used the app to unlock the door, and to increase the temperature of the air conditioner.
